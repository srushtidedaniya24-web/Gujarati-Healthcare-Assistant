1.1 Sentence Segmentation

Divides clinical text into individual sentences by identifying boundary markers (પૂર્ણવિરામ |, ?, !). Handles Gujarati-specific challenges like abbreviations containing periods (e.g., "ડો." for doctor) and decimal numbers in dosages.

2.1 Bag of Words (BoW)

Creates a vocabulary of all unique tokens in the Gujarati clinical corpus and represents each document as a sparse vector of token counts. Disregards grammar and word order to provide fixed-length feature vectors for classification.

Comparison 1 TF vs TF-IDF

Side-by-side evaluation of Term Frequency (TF) and Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) for Gujarati clinical text. lightbulb Reflection Note (1)

Reflection Challenges & Learnings
Challenge: Data Scarcity

Building Gujarati clinical NLP models faced significant data scarcity — limited availability of annotated medical corpora required extensive manual annotation by domain experts and synthetic data generation via back-translation to bootstrap the training pipeline.

Learning: Subword Modeling is Essential

FastText's subword approach proved critical for Gujarati's rich agglutinative morphology. Decomposing compound words like "હૃદયરોગ" (હૃદય + રોગ) into character n-grams recovered ~90% of OOV terms that word-level models would miss, dramatically improving clinical entity recognition.

2.2 TF-IDF

Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency weighs each term by its frequency in a document against its rarity across the Gujarati clinical corpus. Down-weights common stopwords (છે, માં) while boosting rare medical terminology for improved information retrieval.

compare_arrows Comparative Analysis (1)

Comparison 2 Word2Vec vs FastText

In-depth comparison of Word2Vec (CBOW/Skip-gram) and FastText for Gujarati clinical NLP. FastText is the clear winner due to its subword n-gram approach that handles Gujarati's agglutinative morphology — decomposing compound words like "હૃદયરોગ" (heart disease) into known character n-grams from "હૃદય" (heart) and "રોગ" (disease), while Word2Vec would mark them OOV.

lightbulb Reflection Note (1)

Reflection Challenges & Learnings
Challenge: Clinical Accuracy

Medical translation and entity extraction demand near-perfect precision — even minor errors can have serious consequences for patient safety. Achieving clinically acceptable accuracy required multiple fine-tuning iterations with clinician-validated training data and rigorous threshold calibration.

Learning: Domain Adaptation Matters

Pre-trained multilingual models (mBERT, XLM-R) showed poor performance on Gujarati clinical text without domain-specific fine-tuning. Adapting with just 5,000 medical sentences improved F1 scores by 18–25%, proving that domain adaptation is far more impactful than model size for specialized healthcare NLP.
