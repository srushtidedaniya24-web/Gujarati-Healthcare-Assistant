1.1 Sentence Segmentation

Definition

Sentence segmentation divides a block of clinical text into individual sentences by identifying sentence boundary markers such as periods, question marks, and exclamation points. For Gujarati, this involves detecting the પૂર્ણવિરામ (full stop |), while carefully handling abbreviations and medical acronyms that contain periods.

Purpose

To break unstructured clinical narratives into sentence-level units, which is the first step for all downstream NLP tasks. Proper segmentation ensures that each sentence is processed as a coherent linguistic unit rather than a jumbled block of text.

Working Principle

The algorithm scans text character-by-character, identifying boundary tokens (। ? !) while using a rule-based or ML-based disambiguation engine to distinguish sentence-final punctuation from abbreviations (e.g., "ડો." for doctor), decimal numbers, and acronyms common in medical records.

Example
Input: "દર્દીને તાવ છે. તેમને ઉધરસ પણ છે. શું સારવાર આપવી?"
S1: દર્દીને તાવ છે. S2: તેમને ઉધરસ પણ છે. S3: શું સારવાર આપવી?

1.2 Tokenization

Definition

Tokenization segments each sentence into words, subwords, or symbols (tokens). For Gujarati, whitespace and punctuation serve as natural delimiters, but compound words and clitics require special handling for accurate medical term extraction.

Purpose

To convert raw text into a sequence of discrete tokens that serve as the atomic units for vocabulary building, vectorization, and model input. Proper tokenization preserves the meaning of multi-word medical terms and handles Gujarati's agglutinative morphology.

Working Principle

A tokenizer applies whitespace splitting, punctuation-aware segmentation, and optionally subword decomposition (via BPE, WordPiece, or Unigram models). For Gujarati, it must handle compound words like "દવાખાનું" (દવા + ખાનું) and clitics attached to verbs and nouns.

Example
"તાવ" + "આવે" + "છે" → [તાવ, આવે, છે]
Input: "તાવ આવે છે" → 3 tokens

1.3 Noise Removal

Definition

Noise removal strips extraneous, non-informative elements from clinical text that interfere with NLP processing. This includes HTML tags, special characters, URLs, non-medical numbers, and inconsistent whitespace commonly found in digitized medical records.

Purpose

To eliminate artifacts from data digitization, OCR errors, and formatting that add no semantic value but increase vocabulary size and confuse downstream models. Clean data directly improves model accuracy and reduces training time.

Working Principle

Regex-based pattern matching identifies and removes markup tags, special Unicode symbols, URLs, email addresses, timestamps, and extra whitespace. A whitelist approach preserves Gujarati characters, medical symbols, and clinically relevant punctuation while stripping everything else.

1.4 Text Cleaning

Definition

Text cleaning goes beyond noise removal by correcting systematic errors in clinical text. This includes Unicode normalization, spell correction, case normalization, and expanding common medical abbreviations into their full forms for consistent representation.

Purpose

To normalize surface-level variations of the same underlying content so that models treat equivalent texts identically. This reduces sparsity and improves generalization by eliminating orthographic inconsistencies in clinical documentation.

Working Principle

Applies Unicode NFKC normalization to canonicalize Gujarati characters, uses a medical abbreviation dictionary for expansion, runs a spell checker with a clinical Gujarati lexicon, and standardizes digit formats. Each operation is configurable based on the specific clinical domain.

2.1 Bag of Words (BoW)

Definition

Bag of Words creates a vocabulary of all unique tokens in the corpus and represents each document as a sparse vector of token counts. It disregards grammar and word order, focusing solely on term presence and frequency within the document.

Purpose

To transform raw text into a numerical format that machine learning algorithms can process. BoW serves as the simplest baseline for text classification tasks, providing a fixed-length feature vector for each document regardless of its length.

Working Principle

Build a vocabulary D of all unique tokens across the corpus. For each document, create a |D|-dimensional vector where each dimension counts the occurrences of the corresponding vocabulary term. The result is a sparse, high-dimensional matrix of shape (N_docs, |D|).

Example
તાવ1 આવે1 છે1 દુખાવો2 Vocab Size5,000
Input: "દુખાવો થાય છે. તાવ આવે છે. દુખાવો વધે છે."

  • check_circle Baseline model for clinical classification
  • check_circle Sparse representation is memory-efficient

  • remove_circle High-dimensional and sparse
  • remove_circle Common words dominate (no weighting)
Applications

2.2 TF-IDF

Definition

Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) weighs a term by how often it appears in a document (TF) against how rarely it appears across the entire corpus (IDF). This down-weights common words and highlights distinctive medical terminology.

Purpose

To address BoW's limitation of treating all terms equally by assigning higher importance to terms that are frequent in a specific document but rare globally. This makes TF-IDF particularly effective for information retrieval and keyword extraction.

Working Principle

TF(t,d) = (count of term t in document d) / (total terms in d). IDF(t) = log(N / df(t)) where N is total documents and df(t) is document frequency. The final weight is TF(t,d) × IDF(t). Terms with high TF in few documents get the highest scores.

Example
તાવ (fever)TF-IDF: 0.874
શરદી (cold)TF-IDF: 0.652
છે (is)TF-IDF: 0.023
માં (in)TF-IDF: 0.011

  • check_circle Corpus-adaptive importance scoring
Limitations
  • remove_circle Still ignores word order and context
  • remove_circle Sparse high-dimensional representation
Applications

2.3 N-Grams

Definition

N-Grams capture contiguous sequences of N tokens from a given text. Unigrams (N=1) capture single words, bigrams (N=2) capture word pairs, and trigrams (N=3) capture three-word phrases crucial for understanding medical context.

Purpose

To capture local word order and phrase-level meaning that unigram models miss. Medical concepts often span multiple words (e.g., "બ્લડ પ્રેશર" for blood pressure), and N-Grams preserve these multi-word expressions as atomic features.

Working Principle

Slide a window of size N across the token sequence, extracting each contiguous N-token sequence as a feature. These N-Gram features are then counted or weighted (e.g., with TF-IDF) just like unigrams. A common range is N=1 to N=3 for comprehensive coverage.

Example
Unigrams:
દર્દીને, તાવ, અને, શરદી, છે
Bigrams:
દર્દીને તાવ, તાવ અને, અને શરદી
Trigrams:
દર્દીને તાવ અને, તાવ અને શરદી
Advantages
  • check_circle Captures multi-word medical expressions
  • check_circle Preserves Gujarati compound phrase meaning
  • check_circle Configurable N-range for flexible extraction
Limitations
  • remove_circle Exponential feature explosion as N increases
  • remove_circle Data sparsity for higher-order N-Grams
Applications
  • check_circle Clinical phrase extraction, language identification
  • check_circle Medical text classification with phrase-level features

BoW vs TF-IDF vs N-Grams

Each technique serves a different purpose. BoW provides raw term presence; TF-IDF adds corpus-level importance weighting; N-Grams capture phrase-level context. In practice, they are often combined — TF-IDF weighted N-Grams (range 1-3) offer the best balance for Gujarati clinical NLP, yielding ~40% improvement in classification accuracy over BoW alone.

3.1 Word Embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe, FastText)

Definition

Word embeddings map each word to a fixed-length dense vector (typically 100-300 dimensions) in a continuous vector space. Word2Vec (CBOW/Skip-gram) learns from local context windows, GloVe leverages global co-occurrence statistics, and FastText adds character n-gram subword information.

Purpose

To capture semantic and syntactic relationships between words in a dense, low-dimensional space. Unlike sparse BoW vectors, embeddings encode that "તાવ" and "બુખાર" are semantically similar while being different from "હૃદય" (heart).

Working Principle

Word2Vec uses a shallow neural network to predict a word from its context (CBOW) or context from a word (Skip-gram). GloVe factorizes the global word-word co-occurrence matrix. FastText represents words as bags of character n-grams, enabling subword-level understanding critical for Gujarati morphology.

Example
તાવ → બુખાર (Word2Vec: 0.89)
તાવ → શરદી (GloVe: 0.74)
તાવ → હૃદય (FastText: 0.21)
Cosine similarity between Gujarati medical term vectors

Limitations

  • remove_circle Requires large corpus for quality training

3.2 Contextual Embeddings (ELMo, BERT, GPT)

Definition

Contextual embeddings produce different vector representations for the same word depending on its surrounding context. ELMo uses bidirectional LSTMs, BERT uses masked language modeling, and GPT uses autoregressive generation — each yielding rich, context-aware representations.

Purpose

To address the polysemy problem that static embeddings cannot handle. The same medical term (e.g., "તાવ") can mean different things in different contexts — a symptom vs. a treatment response — and contextual embeddings capture these nuanced differences.

Working Principle

Deep neural networks (LSTM or Transformer) encode each token with awareness of all other tokens in the sentence. ELMo concatenates hidden states from bidirectional LSTM layers. BERT uses a Transformer encoder with masked language modeling. GPT uses a Transformer decoder with autoregressive language modeling.

Example
"દર્દીને તાવ છે" → symptom vector
"દવાથી તાવ ઉતર્યો" → treatment response vector
"બાળકને તાવ આવે છે" → pediatric context vector
Same word "તાવ" gets different vectors in each context

Static vs Contextual Embeddings

Static embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe, FastText) assign one fixed vector per word — efficient but blind to polysemy. Contextual embeddings (ELMo, BERT, GPT) produce dynamic vectors that change with context. Using contextual embeddings yields ~35% improvement in NER accuracy over static embeddings alone for Gujarati clinical text.

4.1 Neural Language Models (RNN/LSTM, GRU)

Definition

Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), LSTMs, and GRUs process text sequentially, maintaining a hidden state that captures information from previous tokens. LSTMs use forget/input/output gates to combat vanishing gradients, while GRUs merge gates for efficiency.

Purpose

To model sequential dependencies in language by learning probability distributions over token sequences. These models excel at tasks where word order matters — from language modeling and generation to sequence labeling and classification of clinical text.

Working Principle

At each timestep t, the network takes token x_t and previous hidden state h_{t-1}, producing h_t = f(W·[h_{t-1}, x_t] + b). LSTMs add gated cell states for long-range memory. GRUs simplify to reset and update gates. All process tokens left-to-right, making them inherently sequential.

Example

LSTM3 gates, long-range memory
GRU2 gates, efficient, competitive
Input: "દર્દીને તાવ છે અને ઉધરસ આવે છે" → Hidden state tracks fever + cough co-occurrence across the sequence

4.2 Transformer-based Representations (BERT, GPT, mBERT, XLM-R)

Definition

Transformers replace recurrence with parallelizable self-attention. BERT uses masked language modeling for bidirectional context, GPT uses autoregressive generation, and multilingual variants (mBERT supporting 104 languages, XLM-R supporting 100) extend these capabilities to Gujarati and other low-resource languages.

Purpose

To overcome the sequential bottleneck of RNNs by processing all tokens in parallel, capturing long-range dependencies more effectively. Multilingual variants enable zero-shot transfer — a model pre-trained on 100+ languages can understand Gujarati clinical text without Gujarati-specific training data.

Working Principle

Self-attention computes query, key, and value matrices for all tokens simultaneously. Each token attends to every other token with learned attention weights. Multi-head attention (8-16 heads) captures different relationship types. Positional encoding injects token order information since the model has no inherent notion of sequence.

RNN/LSTM vs Transformer

RNNs process sequentially — each step depends on the previous hidden state (O(n) time). Transformers process in parallel using self-attention (O(1) steps but O(n²) memory). For Gujarati clinical text, RNNs are lightweight (2x faster inference with GRU) and suitable for edge devices, while transformers excel at long-range dependencies and large-scale understanding with multilingual support across 104 languages.
